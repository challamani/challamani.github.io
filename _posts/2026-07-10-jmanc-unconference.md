On **July 10, 2026**, I had the pleasure of attending **JManc Unconference 2026**, hosted at AutoTrader's impressive new office at **No. 3 Circle Square, Oxford Road, Manchester**. The event kicked off around 9am, and it was a fantastic day filled with insightful conversations, thought-provoking sessions, and a real sense of community.

---

## A Day Shaped by AI

Compared to previous years, one thing stood out immediately: **AI dominated almost every conversation**. Whether we were talking about software craftsmanship, Java frameworks, or the future of our profession, AI kept making its way into the discussion — and rightly so.

This shift in the conversation felt significant. A year ago, AI was an exciting topic on the periphery. In 2026, it's **central to how we think about engineering** — our tools, our workflows, our roles, and even our identities as engineers.

---

## Keynote: Staying in the Driver's Seat

The day opened with a compelling keynote by **Clare Sudbery** — [*"The Power of Craft: Staying in the Driver's Seat of AI-Assisted Development"*](https://publish.obsidian.md/clare-sudbery-questions/Event+Resources/Talks/2026/26-07-10+JManc+unconference%2C+The+Power+of+Craft+-+Staying+in+the+Driver%E2%80%99s+Seat+of+AI-Assisted+Development) — which set the tone perfectly. You can also explore her [slide deck](https://speakerdeck.com/claresudbery/the-power-of-craft-staying-in-the-drivers-seat-of-ai-assisted-development) for a deeper dive.

Clare's talk acknowledged something many of us feel but rarely say out loud: AI is bringing up a **dizzying mix of emotions**. Are we being left behind if we don't "vibe code"? Is it even possible to keep up with the disorienting pace of change? It's easy to feel alienated. But her message was ultimately hopeful — approached with a spirit of **curiosity and exploration**, AI can enhance the best parts of our work: experimenting, learning, and solving problems in new ways.

Her core argument: the **core values of great software engineering** — the ones we've relied on for decades — are actually our **greatest advantage** in an AI-assisted world. Professional judgement, craftsmanship, and a human-centred approach aren't liabilities; they're what keep us in the driver's seat.

One line that really stayed with me:

> *AI has a planetary brain, but it lacks real-world wisdom.*

LLMs have absorbed an enormous amount of human knowledge and can produce impressive results when prompted well. But wisdom — the ability to reason from lived experience and real context — is something AI still cannot genuinely replicate. This raises a question we should all sit with: **How do we tame this titan responsibly?**

## AI and the Engineering Culture Question

Across the sessions, a recurring theme emerged: **how is AI reshaping our engineering culture, and how do we protect what matters?**

### Software Engineering Is More Than Writing Code

One session explored how AI impacts software engineers and our future. The consensus was clear — nobody doubts that AI is remarkably capable at generating code given clear requirements and well-crafted prompts. But software engineering has **never been just about writing code**.

The real work of engineering — requirements analysis, system architecture, stakeholder collaboration, integration, production support, and long-term maintenance — remains deeply human. If AI takes on more of the implementation burden, perhaps it frees us to invest more in:

- Designing better, more resilient architectures
- Understanding the broader enterprise context
- Collaborating more effectively with business stakeholders
- Solving complex, ambiguous production challenges

The concern I took away is that as AI accelerates code delivery, there's a risk of undervaluing the engineering fundamentals that AI cannot replace. We need to be deliberate about preserving and celebrating **engineering craft and depth**.

### Running Local LLMs — Practical AI in Action

One session gave a great practical angle: using a local **Qwen model** as a coding assistant. The workflow was clever — use a proprietary model for planning, then hand off implementation to a locally hosted model that can run overnight. This dramatically reduces costs and keeps sensitive source code private. It's a good reminder that thoughtful AI adoption doesn't have to mean surrendering everything to the cloud.

### Java and AI Frameworks

There was also a lively discussion on building AI applications in Java, with frameworks like **Spring AI**, **Embabel**, and **LangChain4j** getting attention. But alongside the excitement, the group acknowledged real challenges: hallucinations, changing model behaviour over time, prompt regressions, and model degradation. These aren't solved problems — and managing them requires genuine engineering discipline.

---

## Is Spring Still Relevant?

A separate session tackled the question head-on: with **Quarkus** and **Micronaut** gaining ground, does Spring Boot still have a place?

The discussion was balanced. Quarkus and Micronaut have real advantages — faster startup times, lower memory footprint. But Spring Boot's enormous ecosystem, community maturity, and deep production trust make it the practical default for most enterprise teams. Once **Spring Data** is embedded across a domain — especially across multiple data sources — moving away is a significant undertaking.

The exception: ultra-low-latency environments like front-office trading systems, where every microsecond matters and lighter alternatives may be worth the trade-off.

## Protecting Engineering Culture

What I reflected on most walking away from JManc 2026 was this: **the conversations we're having about AI are also conversations about identity and culture**.

As AI capabilities grow, there's a real risk that the craft of engineering — the curiosity, the rigour, the mentorship, the accumulated wisdom — gets eroded if we're not intentional. Unconference events like JManc are part of the answer. They create space for engineers to share, challenge, and connect — things no LLM can do for us.

It was also encouraging to see more volunteers stepping up to support the **Manchester Java Community**. Thriving communities need people who are willing to contribute their time and energy, and it's a great sign to see that spirit alive and growing.
